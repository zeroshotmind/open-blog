The [previous post](/blog/svd-singular-value-decomposition) ended on a theorem and a promise: Eckart–Young
guarantees that truncating the SVD to its top components gives the *optimal* low-rank approximation, and the
measured singular-value spectra of trained networks show that their weight matrices are nearly low-rank to
begin with. This post cashes that in. If the matrices we care about really do hide in small subspaces, then
we should be able to store them, fine-tune them, and recover them while only ever touching a few directions.
That single idea — replace a big matrix with a thin product of two small ones — is the common ancestor of
model compression, parameter-efficient fine-tuning, and matrix completion, and it is the reason a curious
hobbyist can fine-tune a seventy-billion-parameter model on a single consumer GPU.

## Why weight matrices are approximately low-rank

The empirical starting point is that the weight matrices inside trained transformers have rapidly decaying
singular values, so their effective rank is far below their nominal dimension. More striking is what happens
during fine-tuning. Aghajanyan, Zettlemoyer and Gupta (2021) measured the **intrinsic dimensionality** of
fine-tuning — the smallest random subspace in which you can optimize and still reach good task performance —
and found it shockingly small: a few hundred to a few thousand directions suffice to adapt a model with
hundreds of millions of parameters. The pretrained representation already lives on a low-dimensional
manifold, and the *change* you need to make to specialize it lives on an even smaller one. That second fact
is the one LoRA exploits, and it is worth keeping separate from the first: the weights might be only mildly
low-rank, but the *update* to the weights is dramatically so.

## Pruning versus factorization

There are two classical ways to shrink a matrix, and they make opposite bets. **Pruning** sets small entries
to zero — unstructured pruning zeros individual weights, structured pruning removes whole rows, columns, or
heads. It is simple and can be very aggressive, but unstructured sparsity destroys the dense matrix structure
that GPUs are built to multiply quickly, so the parameter savings often fail to translate into speed.
**Factorization** takes the other route: replace $W \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ by a product $AB$ with $A
\in \mathbb{R}^{m \times r}$ and $B \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times n}$ for some small $r \ll \min(m, n)$. This
keeps everything dense — two ordinary matrix multiplies in place of one — and the parameter count drops from
$mn$ to $mr + rn = r(m + n)$, which is a large saving exactly when $r$ is small. Pruning throws away
entries; factorization throws away *rank*. For the structure the SVD reveals, throwing away rank is the
better bet, because the energy of the matrix is organized by rank, not by the magnitude of individual
entries.

## Truncated SVD for compression

The most direct application of the previous post is to compress an existing layer. Compute the SVD of its
weight matrix and keep the top $k$ components, $W \approx U_k \Sigma_k V_k^{\top}$. Fold the singular values
into one factor — store $A = U_k \Sigma_k$ of shape $m \times k$ and $B = V_k^{\top}$ of shape $k \times n$ —
and the layer becomes the product $AB$, computed as two cheaper multiplies. Eckart–Young tells you the
reconstruction error in advance, $\lVert W - W_k \rVert_F = \sqrt{\sum_{i>k} \sigma_i^2}$, so the only real
decision is choosing $k$. The standard heuristic is to keep a fixed fraction of the **spectral energy**:
pick the smallest $k$ such that $\sum_{i \le k} \sigma_i^2 \big/ \sum_i \sigma_i^2$ exceeds, say, $0.90$,
$0.95$, or $0.99$. A fast-decaying spectrum reaches $99\%$ energy at a tiny $k$; a flat one never does, which

## LoRA: don't compress the weights, factorize the update

The decisive move of **LoRA** (Low-Rank Adaptation, Hu et al., 2021) is to apply this thinking not to the
weight but to the *change in the weight* during fine-tuning. Freeze the pretrained matrix $W_0$ entirely and
write the adapted weight as the original plus a low-rank correction,

$$
W = W_0 + \Delta W = W_0 + BA
$$

with $B \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}$ and $A \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times k}$ and $r \ll \min(d, k)$. Only $A$ and
$B$ are trained; $W_0$ never receives a gradient. This is the intrinsic-dimensionality result turned into an
architecture — if the update truly lives in an $r$-dimensional subspace, then parameterizing it as a rank-$r$
product loses nothing while training $r(d + k)$ numbers instead of $dk$. Two details make it work in
practice. The factors are initialized so the adapter starts as a no-op: $A$ is drawn from a small random
Gaussian and $B$ is set to zero, so $\Delta W = BA = 0$ at step one and training begins from exactly the
pretrained model rather than a perturbed one. And the update is scaled by $\alpha / r$, a constant that
decouples the effective learning rate from the choice of rank, so that tuning $r$ up or down does not force
you to retune everything else. Because the correction is just an additive low-rank term, after training you
can compute $W_0 + BA$ once and **merge** it back into a single matrix, leaving inference with exactly zero
overhead — the adapted model is structurally identical to the original.

## Why LoRA works, in one sentence

Connecting it back to the [SVD post](/blog/svd-singular-value-decomposition): fine-tuning needs to add a
low-rank matrix to the weights, the SVD says low-rank matrices are exactly products of thin factors, and
LoRA parameterizes that product directly instead of discovering it after the fact. The rank $r$ is the single
knob trading capacity against cost. A useful diagnostic is to take the trained $\Delta W = BA$ and look at
*its* singular values: if they themselves decay sharply, the effective rank of your update is below $r$ and
you over-provisioned; if they are flat out to $r$, the update is straining against the ceiling and a larger
$r$ might help. The companion post [SVD and Low-Rank Approximation: The Math Behind LoRA](/blog/math-llm-svd-lora)
walks through this argument and the spectra in more detail.

## The family of variants

LoRA spawned a small ecosystem, each member attacking a different bottleneck.


<LoRAVariantsTable />

**QLoRA** (Dettmers et al., 2023) goes after the memory of the *frozen* base model rather than the adapters.
Since $W_0$ never receives gradients, it need not be stored in full precision, so QLoRA quantizes it to a
4-bit format (NF4, a grid tuned for the roughly normal distribution of trained weights) while keeping the
small LoRA adapters in bf16. The base is dequantized on the fly during each forward pass and gradients flow
only into the tiny high-precision adapters — enough to fit a 65B-parameter model on a single 48 GB GPU with
quality within a point or two of full-precision fine-tuning.

**DoRA** (Liu et al., 2024) decomposes each weight into a magnitude and a direction, $W = \lVert W \rVert
\cdot W / \lVert W \rVert$, and applies the LoRA update only to the direction while learning the magnitude
separately. Decoupling "how big" from "which way" makes the optimization behave more like full fine-tuning
and tends to stabilize training, especially at small ranks.

**LoRA+** keeps the architecture but observes that the two factors should not share a learning rate; because
$A$ and $B$ enter the product asymmetrically, using a higher learning rate for $B$ than for $A$ measurably
speeds convergence at no extra cost.

**GaLore** (Zhao et al., 2024) attacks a different memory hog. LoRA shrinks the trainable parameters, but
full fine-tuning's real burden is often the optimizer state — Adam keeps two moments per parameter. GaLore
keeps *all* weights trainable but projects the **gradients** into a low-rank subspace (found by periodic SVD
of the gradient) before the optimizer sees them, so the moments are stored only in that small subspace. It
reaches near-full-fine-tuning quality with optimizer memory even lower than LoRA's, at the cost of the
periodic projections.

## Choosing the rank

The rank $r$ is the hyperparameter that matters most, and the typical menu is small: $4, 8, 16, 32, 64$.
Higher $r$ buys expressiveness and more parameters but can converge more slowly and risks fitting noise.
A workable rule of thumb is to start around $r = 8$ for narrow task adaptation, where the update really is
tiny, and reach for $r = 64$ for broad domain adaptation, where you are reshaping the model more
substantially. Then verify empirically: inspect the singular spectrum of the trained $\Delta W$ and, if it
collapses well before $r$, you can safely shrink it. The point of the whole exercise is that you are not
guessing in the dark — Eckart–Young gives you a principled way to read how much rank your update actually
used.

## Nuclear-norm minimization and matrix completion

Low rank is also the right prior for a different problem: recovering a matrix from partial observations, as
in collaborative filtering, where you see a sparse set of user–item ratings and want to fill the rest.
Minimizing rank directly is computationally intractable — rank is a non-convex, discontinuous count of
nonzero singular values — so the standard relaxation is to minimize the **nuclear norm**, the sum of the
singular values,

$$
\lVert W \rVert_* = \sum_i \sigma_i
$$

which is the tightest convex surrogate for rank in the same way the $\ell_1$ norm is the convex surrogate for
sparsity. Candès and Recht (2009) proved the relaxation is not just convenient but correct: under mild
incoherence conditions you can recover a low-rank matrix *exactly* from a number of random entries that
scales with its rank, by solving the convex nuclear-norm problem. It is the same low-rank assumption as LoRA,
applied to filling in missing data rather than learning an update, and it is the theoretical backbone of
modern recommender systems.

## A worked example

Put numbers to the savings on a single transformer weight matrix of size $4096 \times 4096$. Stored in full,
that is

$$
4096 \times 4096 = 16{,}777{,}216 \approx 16.8\text{M parameters}
$$

A LoRA adapter at rank $r = 8$ replaces the trainable update with two factors of shapes $4096 \times 8$ and
$8 \times 4096$:

$$
4096 \times 8 + 8 \times 4096 = 65{,}536 \text{ parameters}
$$

a compression of $16{,}777{,}216 / 65{,}536 = 256\times$. Bump the rank to $r = 64$ and the adapter grows to
$4096 \times 64 \times 2 = 524{,}288$ parameters, still only $1/32$ of the full matrix — a $32\times$ saving.
Across an entire model these factors of hundreds compound into the difference between a fine-tuning run that
needs a cluster and one that runs on a single card overnight. The arithmetic is mundane; what makes it
remarkable is that, thanks to the low intrinsic rank of the update, those few thousand numbers recover almost
all of the quality of fine-tuning all sixteen million.
